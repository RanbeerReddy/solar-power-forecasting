## Thematic Literature Review

### Theme 1: Irradiance and temperature consistently dominate as predictors
Across nearly every study reviewed, solar irradiance (measured as global horizontal irradiation, solar flux, or radiation) emerges as by far the strongest predictor of PV power output, typically showing a close-to-linear relationship with generation (Aouidad and Bouhelal, 2024; Zazoum, 2022). Ambient and module temperature are consistently included as secondary predictors, while variables such as wind speed, wind direction, humidity, and rainfall show weaker and less consistent correlations, though they still contribute marginal predictive value and interact with panel cooling effects (Aouidad and Bouhelal, 2024; Balal et al., 2023). This convergence across independent studies, sites, and climates justifies the feature set (irradiation, ambient temperature, module temperature) selected for the present project.

### Theme 2: No single model family is universally superior
Despite broad agreement on which weather variables matter, the literature does not agree on which algorithm performs best. Essam et al. (2022) rank ANN above Random Forest, Decision Tree, XGBoost, and LSTM; Balal et al. (2023) instead find Random Forest and LSTM jointly outperform Decision Tree, ANN, CNN, and Gradient Boosting; Aouidad and Bouhelal (2024) find Random Forest best in regression but LSTM best once the problem is reframed as classification; and Zazoum (2022) reports Gaussian Process Regression as most reliable. This inconsistency is precisely the research gap motivating a controlled, single-dataset, matched-protocol comparison, since cross-study rankings are confounded by differences in site, climate, feature engineering, and evaluation metrics (Rahimi et al., 2023).

### Theme 3: Ensemble and hybrid methods tend to outperform single learners
A recurring finding is that combining models — whether through bagging, boosting, stacking, or voting — improves robustness relative to any single base learner. Chakraborty et al. (2023) show voting and stacking ensembles outperforming individual bagging/boosting models in Eastern India, while Khan, Walker and Zeiler (2022) report a 10–12% R² improvement from stacking ANN, LSTM, and XGBoost together. Persson et al. (2017) established gradient boosted regression trees as an effective multi-site approach that continues to underpin much of the tree-based forecasting literature. This suggests that, beyond individual model comparison, ensemble and hybrid strategies represent a promising direction — one this project touches on by including both a bagging-based ensemble (Random Forest) and a boosting-based ensemble (Gradient Boosting/XGBoost).

### Theme 4: Evaluation practice often obscures performance at the moments that matter most
Several studies achieve very high aggregate R² values (often >0.95) using standard regression metrics, yet Aouidad and Bouhelal (2024) demonstrate that these same models can still fail badly on peak power values — arguably the most operationally important periods for grid management. This exposes a methodological weakness across much of the literature: headline metrics such as overall RMSE or R² can mask systematic under-performance in specific, high-value regimes (e.g., clear-sky peak generation vs. cloudy/variable conditions). This project directly addresses this gap by explicitly evaluating model error separately under different weather/generation regimes rather than relying solely on aggregate metrics (see RQ3).

### Theme 5: Dataset and preprocessing choices are rarely standardised
Studies vary widely in resolution (5 minutes to hourly), duration (weeks to a decade), geography (Australia, Texas, Eastern India, Denmark), and preprocessing decisions (outlier handling, missing-value imputation, scaling). This heterogeneity is a major reason why algorithm rankings differ from study to study (Rahimi et al., 2023). It reinforces the value of documenting, in full and reproducible detail, every preprocessing decision made in this project's notebook, so that the comparative results obtained here can be interpreted with a clear understanding of the conditions under which they were produced.

### Synthesis: the research gap this project addresses
Taken together, the literature agrees on *which weather variables matter* but disagrees on *which model works best*, largely because no controlled, single-dataset comparison spans classical linear regression, both major tree-based ensemble families (bagging and boosting), and a deep sequence model (LSTM) under one consistent preprocessing and evaluation pipeline, with explicit attention to performance under different weather regimes rather than aggregate metrics alone. This project's comparative design — four models, one dataset, one pipeline, disaggregated error analysis — is constructed specifically to close this gap at a scale appropriate for a mini research project.
